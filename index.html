Comparing & Ordering Decimals

Bigger, smaller, or the same? Place value tells you every time. In this lesson you will compare decimals, put them in order, and place them on a number line.

The big idea

Compare from the biggest place first

Line the numbers up by place value and start at the largest place, on the left. Work right until you find a place where the digits are different. That place decides it.

The number of digits does not tell you the size. A longer decimal can be smaller. If it helps, add a zero on the end to make the lengths match, because a zero on the end of a decimal does not change its value.

Worked examples

1. Work left to right

Compare 3.54 and 3.55. Ones match (3 = 3). Tenths match (5 = 5). Hundredths differ: 4 is less than 5.

3.54 < 3.55

2. Longer is not always larger

Compare 7.03 and 7.3. Make the lengths match: 7.03 and 7.30. Tenths: 0 is less than 3.

7.03 < 7.3   (the longer looking one is smaller)

3. The digit counting trap

Emily says 2.93 must be smaller because it has fewer digits. Hari says 2.891 must be bigger because it has more digits. Both are wrong.

Match the lengths: 2.930 and 2.891. Tenths: 9 is greater than 8, so 2.93 > 2.891.
Careful: counting digits does not work. Compare place by place, starting from the left.

4. A zero on the end changes nothing

Compare 4.3 and 4.30. The extra zero is in the hundredths place and adds nothing.

4.3 = 4.30

5. Ordering is just comparing, again and again

Put 4.3, 4.44, 4.34, and 4.43 in ascending order. Line up the tenths first, then the hundredths.

4.3, 4.34, 4.43, 4.44
Represent it

Place them on a number line

A number line settles any argument. Between 2.89 and 2.94, each small mark is one thousandth. Here are Emily's and Hari's numbers.

2.93 sits well to the right of 2.891, so it is the greater number.
